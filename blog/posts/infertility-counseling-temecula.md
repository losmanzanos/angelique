Infertility is often described in clinical terms — hormone levels, cycle timing, treatment protocols. But anyone who has lived it knows that the experience is anything but clinical. It's grief. It's hope and disappointment cycling month after month. It's watching friends announce pregnancies and feeling a complicated mix of joy for them and sorrow for yourself. It's a relationship with your own body that can start to feel adversarial.

And yet, the emotional dimension of infertility is still one of the most underserved aspects of the experience.

## What Infertility Grief Actually Feels Like

Unlike the grief that follows a concrete loss, infertility is often described as grieving something that hasn't happened yet — a child you imagined, a future you assumed, a version of parenthood you've been mentally building for years. That kind of anticipatory grief is real, and it doesn't get enough acknowledgment.

Common emotional experiences during infertility include:

- Anxiety and hypervigilance around cycles, appointments, and test results
- Depression, especially after failed treatments or pregnancy losses
- Isolation — feeling like no one around you really understands what this is like
- Relationship strain with a partner, particularly when grief is expressed differently by each person
- Resentment or shame that can feel impossible to say out loud
- Difficulty being present in other areas of life because this is consuming everything

None of these responses are weakness. They're the natural result of an experience that is genuinely hard — medically, emotionally, and relationally.

## How Therapy Helps

Infertility counseling at Pivotal Tides offers a consistent space to process what you're going through without having to manage how your support system responds to it. Friends and family, however loving, often default to optimism or advice — which can feel isolating when what you really need is to be heard.

In therapy, we make room for the full complexity of your experience: the grief and the hope, the exhaustion and the resilience, the parts you feel like you should be able to say and the parts you've been keeping to yourself.

I also work with couples navigating infertility together, supporting communication and connection during a process that can put significant strain on a partnership — especially when two people are grieving at different rhythms.

## You're Not Failing

If you've found yourself wondering whether you're handling this "right," or feeling guilty for how much space it's taking up in your life — I want to be direct with you: infertility is one of the most emotionally demanding experiences a person can go through. The space it takes up is proportionate to what it is.
