At some point, most of us received the unspoken message that grief has an appropriate duration. That after a certain amount of time — weeks, months, maybe a year — we should be getting back to normal. That holding on too long is somehow indulgent, or a sign that something is wrong with us.

This is one of the most harmful myths in our cultural relationship with loss.

## What Grief Actually Is

Grief is the natural response to losing something that mattered to you. Most people associate it with death, and death is certainly one of its most common causes. But grief also follows:

- The end of a relationship or marriage
- A miscarriage or pregnancy loss
- An infertility diagnosis and the future it forecloses
- A serious illness — your own or someone you love
- The loss of a job, an identity, or a version of yourself
- Estrangement from a family member
- Leaving a community, a home, or a chapter of life that held deep meaning

Any significant loss can produce grief. And grief, whenever it comes, deserves to be taken seriously.

## Why "Just Moving On" Doesn't Work

When grief goes unprocessed — buried under busyness, minimized by comparison to others' losses, or pushed aside because life has to continue — it doesn't disappear. It tends to show up sideways: as irritability, numbness, difficulty connecting, anxiety without a clear source, or a persistent sense that something is missing.

Therapy for grief isn't about forcing yourself to feel better faster. It's about creating a space where the loss can finally be named, witnessed, and integrated — rather than carried alone and in silence.

## What Grief Therapy Looks Like at Pivotal Tides

Grief work here is gentle, non-prescriptive, and paced entirely by you. There's no model to move through, no stages to complete. What there is: a consistent, non-judgmental space to say whatever you haven't been able to say elsewhere.

That might look like talking about the person or thing you lost — really talking about them, not just the logistics of the loss. It might look like exploring the complicated emotions grief often brings with it: anger, relief, guilt, even gratitude alongside the sorrow. It might look like figuring out, slowly, what a life that carries this loss still feels like.

> Grief is just love with nowhere to go.

And for losses that are still in progress — a loved one in decline, an ongoing medical situation, a relationship that's ending — therapy offers support not just after the loss, but through it.

## There's No Wrong Way to Grieve

Whatever you're grieving, and however long it's been, you don't need to justify it to access support. Grief that's years old is still grief. A loss that others seem to have minimized is still a real loss.
