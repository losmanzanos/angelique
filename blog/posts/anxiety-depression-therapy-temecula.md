Anxiety and depression are, statistically, two of the most common mental health experiences people have. They're also two of the most commonly minimized — both by the people around us and by ourselves.

If you've ever talked yourself out of seeking help because you weren't "bad enough," or because you figured everyone feels this way sometimes, you're not alone in that. But you may also be overdue for support.

## What Anxiety Actually Looks Like

Anxiety isn't always what people picture — a panic attack, a racing heart, an obvious and identifiable fear. More often, it's quieter than that:

- A mind that never fully shuts off, especially at night
- Difficulty making decisions because of constant "what if" thinking
- Avoiding situations or conversations because the anticipation is too uncomfortable
- Physical symptoms — tension, fatigue, headaches, digestive issues — without a clear medical cause
- A persistent sense of dread that doesn't attach clearly to anything specific
- Feeling like you're always waiting for something to go wrong

High-functioning anxiety, in particular, often goes unrecognized because the person managing it appears to be doing fine from the outside. The internal experience is a different story.

## What Depression Actually Looks Like

Depression is more than sadness — though sadness is certainly part of it for many people. It can also look like:

- Emotional numbness, or feeling disconnected from things that used to matter
- Exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions that used to feel simple
- Withdrawing from people and activities without fully understanding why
- Irritability or a low-grade frustration that sits just under the surface
- A sense of going through the motions without feeling present

Depression and anxiety frequently appear together, each amplifying the other in ways that can be difficult to sort out alone.

## When to Seek Therapy

A useful way to think about this: if something is affecting the quality of your life — your relationships, your work, your ability to be present, your sense of yourself — it's worth exploring in therapy. You don't need to be in crisis. You don't need a diagnosis. You need to be affected.

Many people who come to therapy for anxiety or depression later describe wishing they'd come sooner — not because they were in bad shape for too long, but because the relief they found was more significant than they expected.
